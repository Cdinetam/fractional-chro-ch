FAQ on the Fractional CHRO Model

Is a Fractional CHRO only suitable for large companies?

No. SMEs, scale-ups, and growth-stage companies in particular benefit from this flexible C-level model.

How does this differ from interim management?

Interim roles are often transitional. Fractional engagements are longer-term, more strategic, and more deeply integrated into executive leadership.

Does a Fractional CHRO relieve the CEO?

Yes. Strategic people leadership is represented at C-level, while the CEO is relieved in key HR-related decision areas.

What does it cost when there is no HR leadership — and how does that change with a CHRO?

Introduction

Many companies underestimate the costs that arise when no one leads the HR function strategically. These costs do not appear on a single invoice — they are spread invisibly across budgets, departments, and quarters. We call them "costs of doing nothing".

The three cost drivers without HR leadership

1. Turnover & mis-hiring
Without structured recruiting processes, clear role profiles, and targeted retention, turnover increases. In practice, each mis-hire costs around 50-150% of annual salary: recruiting costs, onboarding time, productivity loss during vacancy periods — plus cultural damage that is harder to measure.

2. Compliance & legal risk
Labor law, data protection (FADP/GDPR), equal treatment, termination protection — without someone actively managing these topics, risks accumulate. A single labor-law case can quickly reach five- to six-figure costs.

3. Productivity & culture
Unclear onboarding, missing leadership development, and weak feedback culture measurably reduce employee performance. What is harder to express in Swiss francs is still real: teams without HR guidance lose direction.

Reading example — what a CHRO changes in practice

Starting point: A company with 80 employees has no dedicated HR leadership. Estimated costs of doing nothing are 260k CHF per year — split across turnover (114k), productivity loss (65k), compliance risks (42k), and unstructured recruiting (39k).

With a Fractional CHRO (100k CHF/year):
The CHRO introduces structured hiring processes in year one, establishes a compliance framework, and starts targeted retention initiatives. Result: costs of doing nothing drop by around -70% in year one to approximately 78k CHF. Over the following years, this effect strengthens because systems, training, and processes have cumulative impact. After five years, costs of doing nothing are reduced to a fraction of the original level.

Total cost comparison over 5 years:
No CHRO costs around 1,100k CHF in consequential costs. With a Fractional CHRO (100k/year, +20%/year), total costs are about 530k CHF — savings of more than 570k CHF. A Full-Time CHRO (250k/year) can achieve a similar effect on consequential costs, but total cost is significantly higher — savings versus the Fractional model amount to around 800k CHF over five years.

Conclusion

A CHRO pays off not despite its cost, but because of its impact. The Fractional CHRO is the most efficient model: full strategic impact without the fixed-cost risk of a full-time role. The "cost of doing nothing" does not decrease by itself — it requires someone to intervene actively.
